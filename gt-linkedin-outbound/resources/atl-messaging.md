# Above-the-Line (ATL) LinkedIn Messaging

You write LinkedIn DMs and connection notes for VP, C-Level, and Director personas. ATL targets on LinkedIn behave very differently than ATL targets on email - and very differently than BTL targets on LinkedIn.

## What's Different About ATL on LinkedIn

LinkedIn is a busier inbox for senior people than email is. A C-suite executive may receive 50-200 connection requests per week. They have ruthless filters:

| What they tolerate | What they ignore | What earns a block |
|---|---|---|
| Peer-to-peer brief observation | Generic compliments | Anything that mentions saving 3 hours/week |
| Strategic insight that's actually new | "Quick question" openers | Calendar links in M1 |
| Smart referenced signal | Liking 5 of their posts before connecting | Multi-paragraph DMs |
| One genuine question | Multiple questions stacked | "I help companies like yours..." |

ATL on LinkedIn requires more discipline than email because the visibility is higher (their connections see who connects with them) and the time per message is shorter (they skim for 1-2 seconds).

## Process

1. **Confirm ATL target** - VP, C-Level, Director, or equivalent. If unclear, ask.
2. **Identify strategic angle** - Revenue impact, market positioning, board-level priority, talent/team scaling, competitive risk
3. **Find a real signal** - ATL personas are post-active more often than ICs; this is usually findable
4. **Draft connection note** - 2-3 sentences max, observation-based, no pitch (use note here - ATL appreciates the effort)
5. **Draft M1** - 40-50 words MAX. Strategic language. Zero operational details.
6. **Draft M2** - Soft, smart, never aggressive

## ATL Rules on LinkedIn

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| **40-50 words max for M1** | Executives skim; every word must earn its place |
| **Strategic language only** | "Revenue impact", "competitive positioning", "market timing", "board-level priority" |
| **Lead with outcome, not process** | They care WHAT changes, not HOW it works |
| **No operational details** | "Save 3 hours/week" gets you delegated to a subordinate immediately |
| **Question format outperforms statement** | Prompts strategic reflection: "What would it mean for Q2 if..." |
| **Use the connection note** | Senior personas appreciate the effort; skip-no-note backfires |
| **Max 2 DMs (M1 + M2B)** | Anything past M2 with a senior person reads as desperate |

## What ATL Personas Care About

- Revenue impact (top-line growth, margin expansion)
- Risk reduction (regulatory, talent, market, competitive)
- Strategic initiatives and 12-24 month roadmaps
- Competitive positioning and differentiation
- Board / investor optics
- Their own credibility and career trajectory

## Fatal Mistakes

### 1. Talking operational language to an executive

"Save 3 hours per week per rep" → instant delegation to a Sales Director. Your DM gets forwarded down and you lose the thread. Even if you eventually get a reply, you've lost the senior buy-in.

### 2. Pitching in M1

"I'd love to show you how we help companies like [theirs]" - instant decline. Senior people DM-block faster than they email-archive.

### 3. Generic strategic clichés

"In today's competitive landscape..." / "Driving operational excellence..." / "Synergies and ROI..." - corporate-speak filler. Executives have heard 10,000 of these. Specificity wins.

### 4. Multiple questions

Stacking questions overwhelms a senior person who's reading 5 DMs in 60 seconds. One question. One.

### 5. The "I noticed you're a VP of..." opener

Citing their title is the lowest-effort signal possible. Find something real or use a tension-based opener instead.

## When to Target ATL on LinkedIn

- Deal size > $50K ACV
- Strategic / transformation purchase
- Requires top-down budget approval
- Long sales cycle is acceptable
- Their team is small enough that they're hands-on with the buying decision
- The category is new enough that ICs don't have authority to recommend it

If the buying motion is bottom-up (PLG) → target BTL instead.

## Handling ATL Silence (the "Active but No Reply" Pattern)

ATL prospects exhibit one specific behavior more than any other audience: they accept the connection, post on LinkedIn weekly, engage with peer content actively - and ignore your DM for weeks. This is **State 3** in the 4-State Follow-up Model (see `copywriting/re-engagement.md`).

The instinct on a senior prospect is to nudge with a stronger opener or better insight. Don't. ATL silence almost never means "I missed it." It means "not now, not from this angle." Sending another nudge changes the dynamic from "this person operates in my world" to "this person doesn't read social cues."

What to do instead:

1. **Send M2B once** with a different angle (3-5 days after M1) and stop the active sequence.
2. **Tag the conversation** for monthly review.
3. **Re-engage only on a real signal** - they post about a problem your offering solves, change roles, engage with your or your team's content, or their company has a relevant material event.
4. **Cap re-engagement attempts** at one per quarter for ATL. Anything more frequent damages the relationship - and ATL relationships compound over years, not campaigns.

The single best ATL re-engagement opener is one that references something they posted *that week*, not something you sent them three months ago. See `copywriting/re-engagement.md` for the framing.

## Examples

### Example 1 - CEO of a mid-market SaaS, recent post on Q4 retention

**Connection note (within 300 chars):**
```
Your post on retention being downstream of activation timing - sharp framing.
Most CEOs I talk to focus on the wrong end of the curve. Worth being connected.
```

**M1 (after acceptance):**
```
The post got me thinking - most retention conversations at the CEO level focus on 
intervention (win-back, re-engagement) when the leverage is actually upstream at 
activation timing.

What did the data look like at [Company] when you compared the two cost structures?
```

**M2B (no reply, 4 days later):**
```
Different lens - Q1 board cycles in [industry] are exposing CACs that worked at ARR 
rates of 12 months ago and don't anymore.

Most CEOs are rethinking activation economics, not retention spend, to make the 
new math work.

Worth a 15-min conversation if Q2 board prep is on the calendar?
```

---

### Example 2 - VP Sales, scaling fast post-Series B

**Connection note:**
```
Saw [Company]'s [round] news - that scale-up phase usually exposes pipeline 
forecast gaps that didn't matter at seed. Worth being connected.
```

**M1:**
```
The growth from [X to Y ARR] usually exposes a specific blind spot for the VP role: 
forecast accuracy at the rep level is fine, but roll-up discipline isn't, and the 
board notices in the second quarter, not the first.

How are you staging that for [Company]?
```

**M2B:**
```
Different angle - comp plan changes at [stage] usually invalidate the CRM data the 
forecast was built on, in ways that don't show up for 6 months.

Most VPs find out the hard way at the Q3 board.

Worth a quick conversation on the 1-quarter-ahead view?
```

---

### Example 3 - CTO evaluating new infrastructure

**Connection note:**
```
Saw [Company]'s recent move into [region/product]. That kind of expansion usually 
means architecture debt comes due in 6-9 months. Worth being connected.
```

**M1:**
```
The expansion into [new market/product] is the kind of thing that usually surfaces 
infrastructure decisions a CTO inherits but doesn't get to choose.

Most teams in your phase are deciding whether to refactor for the new load now or 
patch through to the next funding cycle.

Where are you landing on that?
```

**M2B:**
```
Lens shift - the talent pool for [the relevant tech stack] tightened 30% in the last 
6 months. Architecture decisions made today have a hiring tail you can't reverse for 
12-18 months.

Most CTOs aren't pricing that into the build-vs-patch calc.

Useful framework on this if you want it.
```

## ATL Voice Notes & Video DMs

LinkedIn supports voice notes (up to 60s) and video DMs. For ATL, the rules are different than text:

**Voice notes - when to use:**
- The signal is highly specific and you have something genuinely worth saying out loud
- Your tone of voice matches the seniority (calm, measured, peer-to-peer)
- Maximum 30 seconds (under the 60s limit) - same word count discipline as text

**When NOT to use voice notes for ATL:**
- M1 by default - feels too aggressive on a first contact
- If your delivery is tense or rushed (it shows immediately)
- If the script is anything you'd be embarrassed to read aloud

**Video DMs:** Almost never use for ATL. Senior people perceive video DMs as performative and time-consuming to evaluate. Stick to text + voice for this persona.

## ATL Reply Patterns

Senior people reply in patterns. Recognize them:

| Their reply | What it means | What to send next |
|---|---|---|
| One-line, vague ("Sure, send something") | Polite test - they're not engaged yet | Send the most concise possible value (1-page, video, link) - earn the next reply |
| Direct question or pushback | Real engagement | Match their energy - direct answer, no fluff |
| "Loop in [name]" | Delegation | Don't lose the thread - DM the named person, reference the executive |
| Silence after first reply | Test of persistence | One more follow-up max, completely new angle |
| "Not relevant for us" | Legitimate disqualification or auto-decline | Respect it - ask if there's a peer at another company who might be relevant (ONE shot only) |
