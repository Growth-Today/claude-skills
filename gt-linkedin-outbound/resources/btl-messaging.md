# Below-the-Line (BTL) LinkedIn Messaging

You write LinkedIn DMs and connection notes for Managers, Individual Contributors, and End-Users. BTL is LinkedIn's strongest persona - the platform's social context plays beautifully for tactical, peer-level outreach.

## Why LinkedIn Is BTL's Strongest Channel

Compared to email, LinkedIn lifts BTL outreach significantly:

| Dimension | Email to a Manager | LinkedIn DM to a Manager |
|---|---|---|
| Cold inbox saturation | Heavy | Lighter |
| Filter aggressiveness | High | Lower (the connection itself acts as a filter) |
| Identity signal | Email signature, maybe | Full profile, mutual context |
| Tone tolerance | Formal expected | Casual expected |
| Response speed | 1-3 days | Often within hours |
| Reply rate uplift vs cold email | - | +30-50% typical |

Managers and ICs check LinkedIn during work breaks, commutes, downtime. The social context makes them more receptive to DMs that recognize their daily reality.

## Process

1. **Confirm BTL target** - Manager, Specialist, Analyst, Coordinator, Senior IC, Tech Lead. If unclear, ask.
2. **Identify daily pain** - What frustrates them every day? What do they spend time on that they hate?
3. **Find a real signal if available** - BTL personas post less than ATL, so signals are scarcer; use tension-based opener if needed
4. **Decide on connection note** - Default: NO note for BTL (it often reduces acceptance vs no note for this persona)
5. **Draft M1** - 50-70 words. Operational language. Specific time/effort observation.
6. **Draft M2** - Soft CTA + concrete value offer

## BTL Rules on LinkedIn

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| **50-70 words for M1** | Slightly longer than ATL - they read more carefully and want detail |
| **Operational language wins** | "Stop spending 4 hours/week...", "Most [role]s I talk to spend X hours doing Y" |
| **Lead with daily friction** | Specifically what they do every day that they hate |
| **Quantify time/effort** | "70% less time on reporting" lands better than "improved efficiency" |
| **Make them look good to their boss** | BTL personas care about how their work is perceived upward |
| **No note > with note for cold ICs** | Generic notes hurt acceptance with this persona |
| **Casual contractions are fine** | "you're", "it's", "that's" - formality reads as corporate spam |

## What BTL Personas Care About

- Daily pain points (the workflow they hate)
- Time savings (specific hours back per week)
- Workflow improvements that they control
- Making their job easier
- Looking good to their boss / VP
- Career growth and skill-building
- Tooling that other people in their role respect

## Fatal Mistakes

### 1. Strategic language to a Manager

"Revenue impact", "competitive positioning", "board-level priority" → doesn't land. Too abstract. They don't think in those terms. They think in tickets, sprints, weekly reports, daily fires. Talk in their language.

### 2. Vague benefit claims

"Improve efficiency" → ignored. "Cut your weekly reporting from 4 hours to 30 minutes" → engaged. BTL responds to specific, concrete numbers tied to a workflow they recognize.

### 3. Only talking about saving time

"Save 3 hours per week" alone doesn't move them. They need the SO WHAT: "Save 3 hours per week SO YOU can spend that time on [thing they actually want to do - strategy, learning, side projects, going home earlier]." The "so what" is the lever.

### 4. Pretending they're more senior than they are

"Help shape your company's GTM strategy..." → if they're a Coordinator, this feels condescending. Speak to where they actually sit, not where they aspire to be.

### 5. Connecting and immediately pitching the product

The shortest path to a block. M1 must NEVER mention your product or company by name.

## When to Target BTL on LinkedIn

- Product-led growth motion
- User adoption drives expansion
- Bottom-up sale (the IC tries it, then advocates upward)
- Need an internal champion before approaching the budget owner
- Your tool replaces a daily-pain workflow
- Free tier or self-serve trial exists

If the buying motion requires VP-level approval and BTL has zero say → target ATL.

## Examples

### Example 1 - Marketing Operations Manager (signal: posted about a martech stack frustration)

**Connection note (or no note - test both):**
```
Your post on the campaign-attribution mess - most Marketing Ops Managers I talk to 
are stuck patching the same gap. Worth being connected.
```

**M1 (after acceptance):**
```
That post hit. The pattern I see: 80% of weekly reporting time goes into reconciling 
data between [common tool 1] and [common tool 2], and the answer changes depending 
on whose dashboard you trust.

Most Marketing Ops Managers end up spending 4-6 hours every week on reconciliation 
that doesn't change anyone's decision.

Is that the picture at [Company] too?
```

**M2A (they replied):**
```
Recognized that. The teams that fix it usually start with a single source-of-truth 
definition before touching the tools - sounds obvious but almost no one does it.

I have a 1-pager on how a couple of [industry] Ops teams structured the definition 
work. Want me to send it over?
```

**M2B (no reply, 4 days later):**
```
Different angle - most Marketing Ops Managers in [stage] companies are about to lose 
budget for next year because reporting is "good enough" - meaning leadership doesn't 
see the time cost.

The fix is usually showing the hours-saved math BEFORE budget review, not after.

Useful framework on how to do that if you want it.
```

### Example 2 - SDR Team Lead (no specific post signal)

**Connection note:** No note (let the sender's profile carry the weight).

**M1:**
```
Most SDR Team Leads I talk to are losing 5-7 hours per rep per week on manual list 
building - usually because the data tools the company bought 2 years ago don't keep 
up with how reps actually prospect now.

The hidden cost is rep morale: prospecting is the hated part, and time spent on lists 
is time not spent on creative outbound.

How's that playing out at [Company]?
```

**M2A:**
```
Yep - same pattern across most teams. The leads I see solve it usually move list 
building OUT of rep workflow entirely (centralized RevOps or external) and free 
reps to do creative outbound only.

Happy to share how a similar [industry] team structured the handoff. Worth a 20-min 
walkthrough?
```

**M2B:**
```
Different lens - H1 quotas are usually set assuming current rep capacity. If list 
building is eating 5-7 hours/rep/week, you're effectively running at 80% of the 
capacity the quota was set on.

That gap shows up in Q2, not Q1, and by then the team's already behind.

Worth a conversation on the math?
```

### Example 3 - Senior Software Engineer / Tech Lead (signal: certification)

**Connection note:**
```
Saw the [framework] cert - that one usually means the team's making structural 
decisions soon. Building out my engineering network in [industry]. Worth being connected.
```

**M1:**
```
The cert usually means one of two things: the team's about to pick up [framework] 
for a new build, OR they're seeing the writing on the wall for the existing stack 
and want skills before the migration.

Most senior engineers in your spot are stuck deciding whether to push for the migration 
now (risky) or wait for leadership to demand it (slower, less control).

Where's the conversation at internally?
```

**M2A:**
```
That's the harder version of the conversation. The teams I see push migrations through 
usually tie the case to a specific business outcome - performance under load, security 
posture, cost - not to "tech debt is bad."

Got a writeup on how a similar team structured the case to their CTO. Want it?
```

**M2B:**
```
Different angle - the talent pool for [old framework] is shrinking faster than most 
teams realize. Hiring ICs who want to work on [old framework] is getting expensive, 
and the senior people are leaving for [new framework] shops.

The migration timing question has a hidden hiring component most engineering leads 
don't price in until it's late.

Useful framework on this if you want it.
```

## BTL Voice Notes - High-ROI on LinkedIn

Voice notes work well for BTL personas (better than for ATL):
- They feel personal and human, not corporate
- The medium signals "I'm taking 30 seconds for you specifically"
- BTL personas appreciate the reduced cognitive load (listening on a commute vs reading at a desk)

**When to use voice notes for BTL:**
- M2A (continuation after they replied) - feels warm and natural
- Replying to a thoughtful question
- Sharing a quick framework explanation

**When NOT to use voice notes for BTL:**
- M1 (default to text - voice in M1 reads as gimmicky)
- If your delivery is rushed or unclear
- If the message is over 30 seconds - keep it tight

## BTL Video DMs - Use Sparingly

Lemlist's video DM feature works for BTL when:
- The message has a visual component (showing a quick screen, demonstrating a tool)
- The video is under 45 seconds
- The script is genuinely helpful, not promotional

For most BTL outreach, text wins.
