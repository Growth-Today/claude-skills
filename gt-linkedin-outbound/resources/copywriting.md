# LinkedIn Copywriting Frameworks & Principles

You help users write effective LinkedIn DMs, connection notes, voice notes, and InMail using proven frameworks adapted for LinkedIn's social context. Most cold-email frameworks need re-tuning for LinkedIn - the channel is shorter, more conversational, and less tolerant of structured selling.

## Core Differences from Email Copywriting

| Element | Email | LinkedIn DM |
|---|---|---|
| Length | 60-90 words | 40-70 words |
| Subject line | Required, 2-4 words | None - connection note is closest equivalent |
| Tone | Professional | Conversational, human, lighter |
| Pitch tolerance | Low | Even lower - they JUST connected |
| Structure | Trigger + Assumption + Proof + Question | Observation + Develop + One question |
| CTA in M1 | Soft, but allowed | NEVER in M1 - only in M2 |
| Multi-format | Plain text only | Text + voice notes + video + InMail |

## 9 Frameworks Adapted for LinkedIn

### 1. **Observation-Develop-Question (ODQ)** - The LinkedIn default

LinkedIn's most important pattern. Replaces email's Trigger-Assumption-Proof-Question.

**Structure:**
```
[Observation about their world - 10-15 words, NOT a question]
[Develop the observation, show context awareness - 1-2 sentences]
[One genuine question - open-ended, low-friction]
```

**Example:**
```
Most VPs of Demand Gen 4-6 months in are running into the same wall - the campaigns 
the previous person built can't be killed yet (politics), but they're underperforming 
the new bets.

The first 6 months are mostly defending what's already there.

How's that playing out for you at [Company]?
```

---

### 2. **Pattern Interrupt (Adapted)**

Like the email version, but tuned for LinkedIn's social context.

**Structure:**
```
[A counter-intuitive observation that breaks the prospect's expectation]
[Why most people get it wrong]
[One question]
```

**Example:**
```
Most "improving forecast accuracy" projects fail not because of bad data, but because 
they assume sales and marketing share a definition of "qualified pipeline."

They almost never do - and the dashboards lie quietly until Q2 board prep.

How's the alignment piece looking at [Company]?
```

---

### 3. **Tension-Based Opener** - When no signal exists

Lead with a shared tension across their persona/industry/stage. The honest framing of "most people in your spot deal with this" feels less salesy than fake personalization.

**Structure:**
```
"Most [role]s at [stage] are dealing with [specific tension] right now."
[Develop one layer]
[Question]
```

**Example:**
```
Most Heads of RevOps at Series B SaaS are dealing with the same trap: the systems 
they inherited can't be ripped out without breaking forecasting, but the systems 
they need to build can't be funded until forecasting works.

It usually takes 2 quarters to escape.

Where are you on that timeline?
```

---

### 4. **Profile-Signal Hook** - Strongest performance

Reference something specific from their LinkedIn activity (post, comment, role change).

**Structure:**
```
"Saw your [post/comment/move] on [topic] - [genuine reaction]."
[Connect to a related observation or implication]
[Question]
```

**Example:**
```
Saw your post on the SDR efficiency trap - specifically your point that automation 
makes the bad parts faster, not better.

Most SDR Leads I talk to keep adding tools to fix what's actually a process problem.

How's that calculus playing out at [Company]?
```

---

### 5. **Curiosity Gap (LinkedIn version)**

Open a loop that the prospect wants to close.

**Structure:**
```
[A specific, surprising claim about their world]
[Develop it just enough to not give it away]
[Soft question]
```

**Example:**
```
The teams hitting forecast in Q2 right now aren't doing better forecasting - they're 
doing fewer deals at higher value. Counterintuitive, but the math works once you see 
the per-deal cost of forecast error.

Have you looked at the pattern at [Company]?
```

---

### 6. **The "Worth Being Connected" Frame** - For connection notes

For 300-character notes specifically, this is the most reliable closer.

**Structure:**
```
[One observation about them or their company]
[One implication]
"Worth being connected."
```

The phrase "worth being connected" outperforms "let's connect" / "open to connecting" / "I'd love to connect" in tested campaigns. It's confident without being pushy, and frames the connection as mutually valuable rather than asking permission.

---

### 7. **Soft Reframe**

Restate something the prospect probably believes - and offer a small twist.

**Structure:**
```
[Conventional wisdom they probably hold]
[Where it actually breaks]
[Soft question]
```

**Example:**
```
Conventional wisdom in [their industry] right now: invest in retention before you 
invest in acquisition.

The teams I see actually growing are doing the opposite, but only because their 
activation is good enough to make new acquisition pay back fast - most companies 
skip that diagnosis.

How's activation looking on your side?
```

---

### 8. **Manual / Casual Style** - Founder-led profiles

The "this isn't a template" frame. Best for founder-led outbound where authenticity is the differentiator.

**Structure:**
```
[Hyper-specific observation, often slightly imperfect - typos, fragments]
[Honest stake in the conversation]
[Direct question]
```

**Example:**
```
Random one - saw [Company]'s push into [specific market]. Curious how you're thinking 
about pricing for the new segment, because the playbook from [original market] usually 
breaks at exactly that transition.

Working with a few [industry] teams on this and seeing some patterns - happy to share 
if useful.
```

This style only works from a real person's profile (founder, exec). Don't use from rented engine accounts.

---

### 9. **No-Oriented Question** - Re-engagement

Already covered in re-engagement sub-skill. Counterintuitive: easier-to-say-no questions get more replies.

**Structure:**
```
[Acknowledge gap or context]
[What's changed]
"Would it be unreasonable to [low-stakes ask]?"
```

## Universal Copy Rules for LinkedIn

1. **40-70 words for DMs** (M1 and M2)
2. **150-220 characters for connection notes** (under 300 hard cap)
3. **No subject line** (DMs don't have one)
4. **One CTA per DM** - and never a CTA in M1
5. **One question per DM** - never stack
6. **Lead with their world, not your product**
7. **No emojis** in B2B outbound
8. **Read aloud test** - must sound like a peer, not a vendor
9. **Match THEIR tone** - formal if their profile is formal, casual if it's casual
10. **No external links in M1 or M2** - LinkedIn's 2026 algorithm now flags external links in DMs as spam-adjacent. Describe the resource and offer to send it on reply ("happy to share the breakdown if useful"). Drop the actual link only after they ask.
11. **Mid-campaign editing rule** - message *copy* can be edited inside an active HeyReach (or similar) campaign; sequence *step structure* cannot. Plan structure carefully upfront; iterate copy freely.
12. **Never use these phrases:**
    - "I came across your profile"
    - "Just checking in"
    - "Following up on my last message"
    - "Hope this finds you well"
    - "I'd love to connect"
    - "Loved your post" (or any version of opening flattery)
    - "Quick question"
    - "Would love to chat"

## Multi-Format Copy: Voice Notes & Video DMs

LinkedIn supports voice notes (60s max) and video DMs. Both can lift performance - IF used correctly.

### Voice Notes

**Best for:**
- M2A (continuation after they replied) - feels warm and natural
- Sharing a quick insight or framework
- BTL personas (less so for ATL)

**Script structure (under 30 seconds):**
```
[Hi [Name] - quick voice note because [reason]]
[Specific observation or value - one sentence]
[Open question or soft offer]
[Sign off - name]
```

**Example script (BTL Marketing Ops Manager, M2A):**
```
"Hi [Name] - quick voice note because the alignment thing you mentioned is exactly 
where most teams I work with get stuck. There's a 1-page framework I put together 
on how to define stage progression before touching the tools - saves about 6 weeks 
of debate. Happy to send it if useful. - [Name]"
```

**Voice note rules:**
- Under 30 seconds (60s is the limit, but shorter is stronger)
- Real, calm voice - no hype, no salesperson energy
- Reference something specific from their profile or reply
- Always sign off with your name (LinkedIn doesn't auto-name voice notes)
- Test on yourself first - listen back. If it sounds rushed or "salesy", re-record.

### Video DMs

**Best for:**
- A specific visual demo or framework that's easier to show than describe
- Strong existing context (they replied, they engaged)

**When NOT to use video DMs:**
- M1 (cold) - feels performative
- Senior personas (ATL) - they perceive video as time-cost
- If your delivery is awkward (it shows immediately)

**Video script structure (under 45 seconds):**
```
[Address them by name in the first 3 seconds]
[Quick context - why this video specifically]
[Show the thing - screen share, framework, doc]
[Soft offer - "happy to walk through it if useful"]
```

### InMail (Sales Navigator only)

InMail bypasses connection - you can message anyone, even without being connected.

**InMail rules:**
- Treat like cold email, not like a DM (you're not connected, no social warmth)
- 2000 char limit but stay under 500
- Subject line REQUIRED for InMail (unlike DMs)
- Performance: usually 50-70% of DM reply rate (less warm)
- Best use: ATL personas where connection request acceptance is too slow, or competitive accounts

## A/B Testing LinkedIn Copy

Test 2 variations at a time.

| Test type | What to compare |
|---|---|
| Note vs No-note | 50/50 split same audience |
| Signal-based vs Tension-based M1 | Same audience, different opening pattern |
| Short vs Medium DM | 40 words vs 70 words, same content |
| Question vs Statement close | "Curious where you're landing?" vs "Curious where you're landing" |
| Voice note vs text M2A | For prospects who replied to M1 |

Minimum sample: 100 sends per variant. Track BOTH:
- Acceptance rate (for connection note tests)
- Reply rate (for DM tests)
- Reply → meeting conversion (for full-funnel impact)

A note variant with 5% lower acceptance but 2x reply rate is the better choice.

## Sequence Performance Logic

For full sequence structure, timing, and templates → `{SKILL_BASE}/copywriting/examples/dm-sequence.md`

### Performance per touchpoint

**Connection note** - Most replies on LinkedIn DON'T come from notes. The note's job is acceptance, not reply. Optimize for acceptance rate first.

**M1** - Where most replies happen. The MOST important touchpoint to test variations on.

**M2A** - Performance depends on what they replied to M1 with. Reply quality > quantity.

**M2B** - New angle bump. Should add 30-50% lift on top of M1 replies. If not, either M2B angle is too similar to M1, or the original list quality is the constraint.

If no reply after M2 → stop the sequence. Re-engage in 60-90 days with completely new angle (route to **re-engagement**).

## Examples

### Example 1: Framework for DMing a CFO about cost savings

→ Use **Soft Reframe** + ATL tone. Focus on revenue/risk language, not "save money."

```
Conventional wisdom for CFOs in [stage] companies right now is to cut SG&A and 
preserve runway. The CFOs hitting plan are doing the opposite - investing in 
revenue capacity now because the talent reset means hiring later costs 30% more.

How are you weighing the runway vs capacity calculation at [Company]?
```

---

### Example 2: How to structure a 2-message LinkedIn sequence for a SaaS demand gen tool targeting Marketing Ops

→ Use **Tension-Based Opener** for M1 + **New angle** for M2B.

**M1:**
```
Most Marketing Ops Managers I talk to spend 4-6 hours a week reconciling data between 
their CRM and their analytics platform - usually because the two were configured by 
different people 18 months apart.

The annoying part: the time spent doesn't actually change anyone's decision.

Is that the picture at [Company]?
```

**M2B:**
```
Different angle - leadership in most [stage] companies is about to ask Marketing Ops 
to "do more with less" in the H1 budget cycle.

The teams that survive that conversation are the ones with hours-saved math ready 
BEFORE budget review, not after.

Useful framework on this if you want it.
```

---

### Example 3: I sell to e-commerce brand managers, help me write LinkedIn DMs

→ Use **Manual/Casual Style** + BTL tone. E-commerce brand managers respond best to direct, casual, peer-style outreach.

```
Random one - saw [Brand] just relaunched [product line] for Q1. The retention math 
on relaunches usually breaks for the first 6 weeks before it starts working.

Working with a few [vertical] brands on the cohort sequencing for relaunches and 
seeing patterns. Happy to share if relevant - no pitch attached.
```
